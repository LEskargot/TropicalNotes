## Overview: The 6 Dimorphic Fungi (Endemic Mycoses)

Dimorphic fungi exist as **mold in the environment** (25°C) and convert to **yeast in human tissue** (37°C). They are acquired primarily by **inhalation** of environmental spores (except sporotrichosis — traumatic inoculation). All can cause disseminated disease, especially in immunocompromised hosts.

**Key review**: Colombo et al. (2025) *Molecular Aspects of Medicine* — comprehensive overview of all endemic mycoses including blastomycosis, coccidioidomycosis, histoplasmosis, paracoccidioidomycosis, talaromycosis, and implantation mycoses.

**Travel medicine relevance**: Tirado-Sánchez et al. (2020) *Expert Rev Anti-infect Ther* — risk groups include travelers from endemic areas, archeologists, speleologists, and immigrants. Frequently misdiagnosed as TB.

## 1. HISTOPLASMOSIS

### Sources
- Muñoz-Rosa et al. (2025) *Infectio* — overview of epidemiology, clinical features, diagnosis, treatment
- Zida et al. (2024) *J Mycol Med* — systematic review 1992–2021 (212 cases)
- Linder & Kauffman (2019) *Curr Fungal Infect Rep* — epidemiology, diagnosis, clinical manifestations

### Répartition géographique
- **Americas**: Ohio and Mississippi River valleys (USA), Central and South America (especially Brazil, Colombia, Guatemala)
- **Africa**: Intertropical Africa (African histoplasmosis caused by *H. capsulatum* var. *duboisii*)
- **Worldwide distribution** but highest burden in Americas
- Deaths from histoplasmosis in Central/South America may **outnumber deaths from TB** among HIV/AIDS patients (Linder 2019)

### Agent causal
- *Histoplasma capsulatum* var. *capsulatum* (American histoplasmosis)
- *Histoplasma capsulatum* var. *duboisii* (African histoplasmosis)
- Dimorphic fungus: mold in soil (especially enriched with bat/bird droppings) → yeast at 37°C

### Transmission
- **Inhalation** of microconidia from contaminated soil
- Enriched soil with **bat guano** and **bird droppings** (caves, chicken coops, old buildings)
- No person-to-person transmission
- Risk activities: spelunking, construction/demolition, farming

### Incubation
- **3–17 days** (typically 7–14 days) after inhalation
- Can reactivate years later in immunosuppressed patients

### Présentation clinique
- **60–90% asymptomatic** or subclinical
- **Acute pulmonary**: fever, cough, chest pain, malaise, myalgia (self-limited in immunocompetent)
- **Chronic pulmonary**: mimics TB — productive cough, weight loss, cavitary lesions (in patients with underlying lung disease)
- **Disseminated**: fever, weight loss, hepatosplenomegaly, lymphadenopathy, pancytopenia, mucosal ulcers
  - Especially in HIV/AIDS (CD4 <150), transplant recipients, extremes of age
  - High lethality if untreated
- Rare complications: Addison's disease, meningitis, culture-negative endocarditis, hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis (Linder 2019)

### Examen clinique
- Hepatosplenomegaly (disseminated)
- Lymphadenopathy
- Oropharyngeal/mucosal ulcers
- Skin lesions (papules, nodules, ulcers — more common in African histoplasmosis)
- Pallor (anemia)

### Labo
- Pancytopenia (disseminated)
- Elevated LDH, ferritin
- Elevated liver enzymes
- Adrenal insufficiency markers (disseminated)

### DX
- **Histoplasma urinary antigen** (rapid, sensitivity 90%+ in disseminated) — not universally available
- **Antibody testing**: immunodiffusion (ID) and complement fixation (CF)
- Combined antigen + antibody testing improves accuracy (Linder 2019)
- **Culture**: gold standard but takes 2–4 weeks
- **Histopathology**: intracellular yeast in macrophages (GMS/PAS stain)
- **Molecular**: PCR available but not standardized everywhere

### DD
- Tuberculosis (most common misdiagnosis)
- Other endemic mycoses (coccidioidomycosis, blastomycosis, paracoccidioidomycosis)
- Sarcoidosis
- Lymphoma
- Pneumocystis pneumonia (in HIV)
- Community-acquired pneumonia

### Traitement
- **Mild-moderate acute pulmonary**: often self-limited, itraconazole if symptoms >4 weeks
- **Moderate-severe/disseminated**:
  - Induction: **Amphotericin B** (liposomal) for 2–4 weeks
  - Maintenance: **Itraconazole** for ≥12 months
- Monitor antigenuria for treatment response and relapse prevention
- Secondary prophylaxis in HIV until immune reconstitution (CD4 >150 for ≥6 months)

## 2. TALAROMYCOSIS (Penicilliosis)

### Sources
- Wang et al. (2023) *Medical Mycology* — HIV+ vs HIV- comparison (99 patients, southern China)
- Ying et al. (2020) *HIV Medicine* — 1,079 patients, Guangdong, China 2011–2017
- Wu et al. (2025) *Frontiers in Medicine* — HIV-negative patients, mNGS diagnosis

### Répartition géographique
- **Southeast Asia**: Thailand, Vietnam, southern China (Guangdong, Guangxi, Yunnan), Laos, Myanmar, Cambodia, northeastern India
- Incidence increasing — correlated with HIV burden
- Seasonal: peaks during **rainy season** (March–August) (Ying 2020)

### Agent causal
- *Talaromyces marneffei* (formerly *Penicillium marneffei*)
- Only dimorphic species in the *Talaromyces* genus
- Temperature-dependent: mold at 25°C → yeast with characteristic **transverse septum** at 37°C
- Natural reservoir: bamboo rats (*Rhizomys* spp.)

### Transmission
- **Inhalation** of conidia from contaminated soil/environment
- Association with bamboo rat habitats
- No confirmed person-to-person or animal-to-person transmission
- Rainy season exposure (possibly soil disturbance)

### Incubation
- Not precisely established; estimated **1–3 weeks** in acute exposure
- Can reactivate in immunosuppressed patients years after exposure

### Présentation clinique
- **In HIV+ patients** (classic presentation, CD4 typically <100, median 9 cells/μL):
  - Fever (85.6%), lymphadenopathy (72.3%), respiratory symptoms (60.8%)
  - Weight loss (49.8%), skin lesions (44.5%), GI symptoms (44.3%)
  - Skin: **umbilicated papules** (molluscum-like) on face, trunk, extremities — present in <50% (Ying 2020)
  - Disseminated disease with blood/bone marrow/lymphatic/GI/CNS involvement (69%)
  - GI bleeding (33%)
- **In HIV- patients**:
  - Less dissemination (36%), less skin involvement (21%)
  - Productive cough and fever predominant
  - Increasingly recognized in non-HIV immunocompromised (anti-CD20, transplant, autoimmune)
- **Case fatality**: ~20% in both groups with treatment (Wang 2023)

### Examen clinique
- Umbilicated papular skin lesions (face, ears, trunk)
- Generalized lymphadenopathy
- Hepatosplenomegaly
- Oral lesions (palate)
- Wasting

### Labo
- **Hypoalbuminemia** (98.6%), **anemia** (95.6%)
- Elevated AST (76.9%), elevated ALP (55.8%)
- Thrombocytopenia (53.7%)
- Elevated ALT (higher in HIV+)
- Pancytopenia in severe disease

### DX
- **Blood culture**: positive in 66.6% (Ying 2020)
- **Bone marrow culture**: positive in 74.5%; combined blood + BM → 86.6%
- Culture on Sabouraud: mold at 25°C with diffusible red pigment; yeast at 37°C
- **Histopathology**: intracellular yeast with central septum (Wright/Giemsa stain of skin/BM)
- **mNGS**: 100% positivity vs 10% for conventional testing (Wu 2025) — emerging rapid diagnostic
- Serology: galactomannan (cross-reaction with Aspergillus assays)

### DD
- Tuberculosis (most common misdiagnosis)
- Disseminated cryptococcosis
- Disseminated histoplasmosis
- Molluscum contagiosum (skin lesions)
- Lymphoma
- Leishmaniasis

### Traitement
- **Severe/disseminated**:
  - Induction: **Amphotericin B** (liposomal) for 2 weeks
  - Consolidation: **Itraconazole** 400mg/day for 10 weeks
  - Maintenance: **Itraconazole** 200mg/day
- **Azole monotherapy** alone is associated with **higher mortality** (Ying 2020)
- Voriconazole: effective alternative, especially in HIV-negative patients (Wu 2025)
- In HIV+: initiate ART after 2 weeks of antifungal therapy
- Poor ART adherence, advanced age, EBV co-infection → independent mortality risk factors (Wang 2023)

## 3. COCCIDIOIDOMYCOSIS (Valley Fever)

### Sources
- Crum (2022) *Infect Dis Ther* — comprehensive contemporary review (82 citations)
- Herrick et al. (2020) *Am Fam Physician* — primary care perspective
- Zaheri et al. (2023) *Cureus* — pathogenesis and treatment

### Répartition géographique
- **Southwestern USA**: Arizona, California (San Joaquin Valley), Nevada, New Mexico, Utah, Texas
- **Mexico**, Central America
- **South America**: Venezuela, Colombia, Argentina, Paraguay, Brazil (semi-arid regions)
- Geographic range **expanding** — now reported in eastern Washington, Oregon (Crum 2022)
- Climate change and population growth driving increased incidence

### Agent causal
- *Coccidioides immitis* (California, Baja) and *Coccidioides posadasii* (rest of range)
- Dimorphic: **arthroconidia** in soil → **spherules** containing endospores in tissue (unique lifecycle)
- Found in arid/semi-arid alkaline soils

### Transmission
- **Inhalation** of arthroconidia made airborne by:
  - Natural events: earthquakes, dust storms, fires, wind
  - Human activities: construction, agriculture, military exercises, recreation
- **No person-to-person transmission** (except rare wound/transplant)
- Leading cause of CAP in endemic areas (17–29% of cases) (Herrick 2020)

### Incubation
- **1–4 weeks** (typically 7–21 days)

### Présentation clinique
- **~60% asymptomatic** (Crum 2022)
- **Primary pulmonary** (~40% of infections):
  - "Desert rheumatism": fever, erythema nodosum, arthralgias (classic triad — Zaheri 2023)
  - Cough, pleuritic chest pain, fatigue, night sweats
  - Usually self-limited in 2–6 weeks
- **Chronic pulmonary**: cavitary disease, nodules
- **Disseminated** (0.5–2%):
  - Skin (papules, nodules, verrucous plaques, abscesses)
  - Bones/joints (vertebrae, long bones)
  - **Meningitis** (most feared — requires lifelong treatment)
  - Risk factors: immunodeficiency (HIV, transplant, pregnancy), African American and Filipino descent

### Examen clinique
- Erythema nodosum (shins)
- Erythema multiforme
- Skin lesions (disseminated)
- Joint effusion
- Meningismus (meningitis)

### Labo
- Often nonspecific
- Eosinophilia (suggestive finding)
- Elevated ESR/CRP
- CSF: lymphocytic pleocytosis, low glucose, elevated protein (meningitis)

### DX
- **Serology**: IgM (EIA) → confirm with **immunodiffusion** (ID)
- **Complement fixation (CF)** titer: correlates with disease burden; rising titer = dissemination
- Serology may be negative for 1–3 weeks after onset (Herrick 2020)
- **Culture**: definitive but biohazard (BSL-3); white fluffy mold with barrel-shaped arthroconidia
- **Histopathology**: large spherules (20–200 μm) containing endospores (pathognomonic)
- **Molecular**: PCR increasingly available

### DD
- Community-acquired pneumonia (bacterial)
- Tuberculosis
- Lung cancer / lymphoma
- Other endemic mycoses (histoplasmosis, blastomycosis)
- Sarcoidosis

### Traitement
- **Mild acute pulmonary in healthy host**: observation, symptomatic treatment (most resolve spontaneously)
- **Risk factors for complicated disease or symptomatic >6 weeks**:
  - **Fluconazole** 400–800 mg/day or **itraconazole** 200 mg BID
  - Duration: 3–12 months
- **Severe pulmonary / disseminated**:
  - **Amphotericin B** (lipid formulation) initially, then step-down to azole
- **Meningitis**: **fluconazole** 400–1000 mg/day — **lifelong therapy**
- Monitor CF titers for 2+ years after treatment (Herrick 2020)
- No vaccine currently available; dust avoidance in endemic areas

## 4. PARACOCCIDIOIDOMYCOSIS

### Sources
- Peçanha et al. (2022) *J Fungi* — comprehensive review (47 citations)
- Peçanha-Pietrobom et al. (2023) *J Fungi* — pulmonary diagnosis and treatment
- Wagner et al. (2021) *J Fungi* — 83 cases diagnosed in Europe (travel medicine relevance)

### Répartition géographique
- **Latin America exclusively**: Brazil (80% of cases), Colombia, Venezuela, Ecuador, Argentina, Paraguay
- Brazil: incidence rising in **Amazon region** (deforestation), decreasing in south (urbanization)
- Infection acquired in rural areas but symptoms may appear **years to decades later** — can present in non-endemic countries (Wagner 2021: 83 cases diagnosed in 11 European countries)

### Agent causal
- *Paracoccidioides brasiliensis* complex (4 phylogenetic species) and *Paracoccidioides lutzii*
- Thermodimorphic: mold in soil → **large yeast with multiple buds** ("ship's pilot wheel" appearance) at 37°C

### Transmission
- **Inhalation** of conidia from contaminated soil
- Associated with agricultural work, soil disturbance in humid tropical/subtropical areas
- Predominantly affects **male agricultural workers** (estrogen may be protective — 10–15:1 M:F ratio)
- No person-to-person transmission

### Incubation
- **Extremely variable**: weeks to **years or decades** (reactivation disease)
- Acute/subacute form: weeks to months (children, young adults)
- Chronic form: years to decades after initial exposure

### Présentation clinique
- **Acute/subacute form** (juvenile type, ~5–10%):
  - Children and young adults (<30 years)
  - Rapid dissemination: lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly, bone marrow involvement
  - Fever, weight loss, anemia
- **Chronic form** (adult type, ~90%):
  - Adults 30–60 years, mostly male agricultural workers
  - **Pulmonary**: chronic cough, dyspnea (bilateral interstitial infiltrates — "butterfly wing" pattern)
  - **Mucosal**: mulberry-like (moriform) stomatitis, oropharyngeal ulcers, laryngeal involvement
  - **Skin**: papules, nodules, ulcerated lesions
  - **Adrenal**: insufficiency in ~40% of disseminated cases
  - **CNS**: intraparenchymal lesions (rare)
- Frequently **misdiagnosed as tuberculosis** (Peçanha-Pietrobom 2023)

### Examen clinique
- Moriform (mulberry-like) oral/oropharyngeal ulcers (pathognomonic appearance)
- Cervical/generalized lymphadenopathy
- Hepatosplenomegaly (acute form)
- Adventitious lung sounds (chronic)
- Skin lesions (face, perioral)
- Signs of adrenal insufficiency (hypotension, hyperpigmentation)

### Labo
- Elevated inflammatory markers
- Eosinophilia (chronic form)
- Anemia
- Hypoalbuminemia
- Adrenal insufficiency labs (cortisol, ACTH)
- Imaging: bilateral interstitial infiltrates, adrenal calcification/enlargement

### DX
- **Direct microscopy**: KOH/Giemsa — **"ship's pilot wheel"** yeast (multiple buds, 15–30 μm) — mainstay even at reference centers (Peçanha 2022)
- **Culture**: slow growth (4–6 weeks)
- **Serology**: immunodiffusion — gp43 antigen (high sensitivity/specificity)
- **Histopathology**: granulomas with multipolar budding yeast (GMS/PAS)
- Molecular techniques exist but remain limited

### DD
- Tuberculosis (main differential)
- Histoplasmosis
- Squamous cell carcinoma (oral lesions)
- Lymphoma
- Leishmaniasis (mucosal)
- Wegener's granulomatosis
- Inflammatory bowel disease (intestinal form — Cruz 2021)

### Traitement
- **Mild-moderate**: **Itraconazole** 200 mg/day for 9–18 months (first-line)
- **Alternative**: **Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (TMP-SMX / Cotrimoxazole)** — longer treatment (18–24 months) but widely available and cheap
- **Severe/disseminated**: **Amphotericin B** (induction) → itraconazole (maintenance)
- New azoles (voriconazole, posaconazole) may be alternatives when standard drugs not tolerated (Peçanha 2022)
- Treatment failure/relapse monitoring with serology

## 5. SPOROTRICHOSIS

### Sources
- Rodrigues et al. (2022) *J Fungi* — epidemiology, diagnosis, treatment progress (102 citations)
- Ríos López et al. (2023) *Int J Med Sci Clin Res Studies* — comprehensive review
- Ramírez-Soto et al. (2025) *Medical Mycology* — osteoarticular forms systematic review

### Répartition géographique
- **Worldwide** (most cosmopolitan dimorphic fungus)
- Hyperendemic: **Brazil** (massive cat-transmitted epidemic, ongoing since 1998), Peru, Mexico, India, China, Japan, South Africa, Australia
- Temperate and tropical climates
- *S. brasiliensis* predominant in Brazilian cat-transmitted outbreaks; *S. schenckii* and *S. globosa* cosmopolitan (Rodrigues 2022)

### Agent causal
- *Sporothrix schenckii* complex (multiple species):
  - *S. schenckii* sensu stricto (cosmopolitan)
  - *S. brasiliensis* (Brazil — most virulent, cat-transmitted)
  - *S. globosa* (Asia, cosmopolitan)
  - *S. luriei* (rare, South Africa)
- Dimorphic: mold in environment (soil, vegetation, sphagnum moss) → cigar-shaped yeast in tissue

### Transmission
- **Traumatic inoculation** (unique among dimorphic fungi — not primarily inhaled):
  - Thorn pricks, splinters, plant matter
  - Soil contact through skin wounds
- **Zoonotic (cat-transmitted)**: scratches/bites from infected cats — major route in Brazil
  - *S. brasiliensis* epidemic in cats → human transmission (One Health concern)
- Rare: inhalation (pulmonary sporotrichosis)
- Occupational: gardeners, florists, farmers, miners, veterinarians

### Incubation
- **1–12 weeks** (typically 1–4 weeks after traumatic inoculation)

### Présentation clinique
- **Lymphocutaneous** (most common, ~70–80%):
  - Painless papule/nodule at inoculation site → ulcerates
  - **Ascending nodular lymphangitis** (chain of nodules along lymphatic drainage) — classic presentation
- **Fixed cutaneous**: single plaque/nodule without lymphangitic spread
- **Disseminated cutaneous**: multiple skin lesions (immunocompromised)
- **Pulmonary**: rare, from inhalation; mimics TB/lung cancer
- **Osteoarticular**: arthritis, osteomyelitis — especially immunocompromised (Ramírez-Soto 2025)
  - Localized: arthritis predominant; systemic: osteomyelitis predominant
  - HIV and cancer are independent risk factors for systemic form
- **Disseminated/systemic**: meningitis, multi-organ — primarily in HIV/immunosuppressed

### Examen clinique
- Papule → ulcerated nodule at inoculation site
- Ascending subcutaneous nodules along lymphatic vessels (sporotrichoid pattern)
- Regional lymphadenopathy
- Joint swelling/effusion (osteoarticular)

### Labo
- Usually unremarkable in cutaneous forms
- Elevated inflammatory markers in disseminated
- Joint aspirate: inflammatory fluid

### DX
- **Culture**: gold standard — mold at 25°C (hyaline hyphae with flower-like conidia); conversion to yeast at 37°C
- **Histopathology**: granulomatous inflammation; organisms difficult to see (cigar-shaped yeast, asteroid bodies) — often need PAS/GMS
- **Molecular**: PCR — main pillar of modern diagnosis; multiplex assays with high sensitivity (Rodrigues 2022)
- Speciation important for epidemiology and treatment guidance

### DD
- Nocardiosis (sporotrichoid pattern)
- Mycobacterial infection (M. marinum, M. kansasii)
- Leishmaniasis (cutaneous)
- Tularemia
- Cat-scratch disease
- Squamous cell carcinoma
- Pyoderma gangrenosum

### Traitement
- **Lymphocutaneous/fixed cutaneous**:
  - **Itraconazole** 200 mg/day for 3–6 months (first-line)
  - Alternative: **saturated solution of potassium iodide (SSKI)** — cheap, effective
  - Terbinafine: emerging evidence
- **Severe/disseminated/pulmonary/osteoarticular**:
  - **Amphotericin B** (induction) → itraconazole (maintenance)
  - Combination antifungals ± surgery for osteoarticular (Ramírez-Soto 2025)
- Emerging resistance to azoles and polyenes reported (Rodrigues 2022)
- Cat infection control critical for epidemic management in Brazil

## 6. BLASTOMYCOSIS

### Sources
- Schwartz et al. (2020) *Clin Infect Dis* — Africa/Middle East review (172 patients)
- Pullen et al. (2022) *J Fungi* — updated review (25 citations)
- Cuddapah et al. (2023) *Cureus* — case report and literature review

### Répartition géographique
- **North America**: Mississippi/Ohio River valleys, Great Lakes region (USA and Canada)
- **Africa**: widespread (25 countries), predominantly *B. percursus* and *B. emzantsi* (Schwartz 2020)
- **Middle East, India** (to lesser extent)
- Geographic distribution likely **underestimated** due to limited reporting (Pullen 2022)
- Multiple species now recognized: *B. dermatitidis*, *B. gilchristii*, *B. percursus*, *B. emzantsi*

### Agent causal
- *Blastomyces dermatitidis* and *Blastomyces gilchristii* (North America)
- *Blastomyces percursus* (Africa — 65% of genotyped isolates, Schwartz 2020)
- *Blastomyces emzantsi* (South Africa)
- Dimorphic: mold in moist soil near waterways → **broad-based budding yeast** (8–15 μm) at 37°C

### Transmission
- **Inhalation** of conidia from moist soil near waterways, decaying wood
- Associated with outdoor activities near water: fishing, hunting, camping
- **No person-to-person transmission**
- Can infect dogs (sentinel animals)

### Incubation
- **3–15 weeks** (median ~45 days; wide range reported — Cuddapah 2023)
- Prolonged incubation possible

### Présentation clinique
- **~50% asymptomatic** (Pullen 2022)
- **Pulmonary** (most common symptomatic):
  - Acute pneumonia: fever, cough, myalgia, arthralgia
  - Chronic: mimics TB or lung cancer
  - Rapidly progressive/ARDS (immunocompromised)
- **Extrapulmonary** (25–40%):
  - **Skin**: verrucous (wart-like) or ulcerative lesions, often face and extremities
    - In Africa: cutaneous disease predominant (73%) with distinct patterns (Schwartz 2020)
  - **Bones/joints**: osteolytic lesions, arthritis (48% in African cases)
  - **Genitourinary**: prostatitis, epididymo-orchitis
  - **CNS**: meningitis, brain abscess (rare, severe)
- African blastomycosis: more cutaneous/osteoarticular; unusual histopathology with short hyphal fragments in tissues (18%) (Schwartz 2020)

### Examen clinique
- Verrucous or ulcerative skin lesions
- Pulmonary findings (consolidation, effusion)
- Bone tenderness
- Prostate enlargement/tenderness (genitourinary)

### Labo
- Often nonspecific
- Elevated WBC, ESR/CRP
- Imaging: mass-like consolidation, nodules (can mimic malignancy)

### DX
- **Culture**: gold standard — white mold at 25°C, broad-based budding yeast at 37°C (2–4 weeks)
- **Direct microscopy**: KOH prep or cytology — **broad-based budding yeast** (refractile, thick-walled, 8–15 μm, single bud with wide base) — pathognomonic
- **Histopathology**: granulomatous/pyogranulomatous inflammation with broad-based budding yeast
- **Blastomyces antigen** (urine/serum): rapid, but cross-reacts with Histoplasma
- **Serology**: limited sensitivity and specificity
- **Molecular**: PCR available at reference labs

### DD
- Tuberculosis
- Lung cancer (mass-like lesion)
- Other endemic mycoses (histoplasmosis, coccidioidomycosis)
- Bacterial pneumonia
- Skin: squamous cell carcinoma, pyoderma gangrenosum
- Bone: bacterial osteomyelitis

### Traitement
- **Mild-moderate pulmonary/extrapulmonary**: **Itraconazole** 200 mg BID for 6–12 months (standard)
- **Severe pulmonary / disseminated / CNS**: **Amphotericin B** (lipid formulation) induction → itraconazole maintenance
- Other azoles: limited data (Pullen 2022)
- Corticosteroids occasionally used in severe disease (limited evidence)
- **CNS disease**: high-dose fluconazole or voriconazole may be considered (better CNS penetration than itraconazole)

## Cross-cutting Themes for All Articles

### Common features across endemic mycoses:
1. **Frequently misdiagnosed as TB** — travel/exposure history is critical
2. **Immunosuppression** (especially HIV/AIDS) dramatically increases risk of dissemination and mortality
3. **Treatment backbone**: Amphotericin B (severe) → Itraconazole (mild-moderate and maintenance)
4. **Culture remains gold standard** but slow; rapid diagnostics (antigen, PCR, mNGS) are improving
5. **Climate change and deforestation** are expanding geographic ranges
6. **Travel history** is essential — symptoms may appear years after leaving endemic area (especially PCM)

### Suggested article prioritization for TropicalNotes:
1. **Histoplasmosis** — most globally relevant, highest burden
2. **Coccidioidomycosis** — expanding range, common in travelers to Americas
3. **Talaromycosis** — critical for SE Asia travelers, HIV patients
4. **Paracoccidioidomycosis** — Latin America endemic, long latency important for European clinicians
5. **Sporotrichosis** — unique transmission (traumatic), cat epidemic relevant
6. **Blastomycosis** — North America + Africa, expanding knowledge
